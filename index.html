Timeline

Explore the inspired beginnings of the church.

  • Spring 1820

    The First Vision

    14-year-old Joseph Smith prayed in a grove of trees near his home seeking guidance about which church to join. In response, he experienced a vision in which God the Father and Jesus Christ appeared to him. They told him that none of the existing churches were true and that he should wait for further instruction.

  • Spring 1828 - Spring 1829

    The Translation of the Book of Mormon

    Joseph Smith translated the Book of Mormon from golden plates he received from an angel named Moroni. Using divine guidance, he dictated the text to scribes, including Oliver Cowdery. The translation was completed in about three months and published in 1830 as a record of ancient prophets in the Americas.

  • Spring 1829

    The Restoration of the Priesthood

    In 1829, Peter, James, and John appeared to Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery as heavenly messengers and restored the Melchizedek Priesthood. This gave them authority to lead the Church, confer the gift of the Holy Ghost, and perform sacred ordinances—marking a key moment in the Restoration.

  • Spring 1830

    The Foundation of the Church

    On April 6, 1830, Joseph Smith formally organized The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in Fayette, New York. With a small group of believers, he followed divine instruction to establish the Church, restoring Christ's original gospel and authority on earth.

  • Winter 1833

    The Establishment of Zion

    The Saints moved to Independence, Missouri, with the hope of establishing Zion—a holy city where they could gather in peace and righteousness. Guided by revelation, they saw this land as the center place of the Lord's kingdom on earth. However, rising tension and persecution from local residents eventually forced them to leave, scattering the Saints and delaying the building of Zion.

  • Spring 1836

    The Dedication of the Kirtland Temple

    The Kirtland Temple was the first temple built by The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, set apart as a holy place for worship and revelation. Following its dedication, Moses, Elias, and Elijah appeared (as well as the Savior Himself), restoring vital priesthood keys and confirming the temple's role in the ongoing restoration of Christ's gospel.

  • Spring 1839

    The Growth, Glory, and Departure at Nauvoo

    In Nauvoo, the Saints found refuge along the Mississippi River, transforming swampy land into a thriving city. Under Joseph Smith's leadership, they built a temple, organized Church offices, and introduced gospel principles like eternal marriage and proxy baptisms. Though Nauvoo was a place of growth and revelation, rising opposition led to renewed persecution and the Saints' westward exodus.

  • Spring 1842

    The Organization of the Relief Society

    In 1842, the Relief Society was organized in Nauvoo under the direction of Joseph Smith, with Emma Smith as its first president. Established to provide service, strengthen faith, and support those in need, the organization gave women a formal role in the work of the Church. From its beginnings as a small group of dedicated sisters, the Relief Society grew into a worldwide force for good, rooted in charity and discipleship.

  • Summer 1844

    The Martyrdom of the Smith Brothers

    Joseph and Hyrum Smith were killed by a mob on June 27, 1844, while held in Carthage Jail in Illinois. Their deaths were a devastating blow to the Saints, who saw them as prophets and leaders chosen by God. Though their lives were taken, their testimonies of the restored gospel stood firm, becoming a source of strength and commitment for the Saints in the trials that followed.

  • Winter 1846

    Succession and Exodus

    After the death of Joseph Smith, questions arose about who should lead the Church. Brigham Young, as President of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles, was sustained by the Saints as the new leader. Under his direction, the Saints prepared to leave Nauvoo and begin a long journey west. In early 1846, they crossed the Mississippi River, facing hardship and sacrifice as they sought a new home where they could worship freely and build Zion in the wilderness.

  • Winter 1853

    The Groundbreaking of the Salt Lake Temple

    Shortly after arriving in the Salt Lake Valley, the Saints selected a central spot to build a temple, a symbol of their faith and permanence. The groundbreaking for the Salt Lake Temple took place on February 14, 1853, under the direction of Brigham Young. Though it would take 40 years to complete, the temple's foundation marked the beginning of a monumental effort to establish a holy place where sacred ordinances could unite families and strengthen the growing Church.

Joseph Smith, Jr.

Inspired. Courageous. Visionary.

Joseph Smith was the first prophet of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Born in 1805 in Vermont, he is best known for translating the Book of Mormon, which was revealed to him by God through an angel named Moroni. Smith organized the Church in 1830 and taught that it was a restoration of the original gospel of Jesus Christ. Despite facing intense opposition and persecution, he led the early Saints through periods of growth and migration before being killed by a mob in 1844 in Carthage, Illinois. His legacy continues to shape the beliefs and structure of the Church today.

Emma Smith

Faithful. Compassionate. Noble.

Emma Smith was the wife of Joseph Smith and an important figure in the early history of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Born in 1804 in Pennsylvania, she played a vital role in the translation of the Book of Mormon, often acting as a scribe and providing support during times of hardship. Emma was also the first president of the Relief Society, the Church's women's organization, and was known for her strength, compassion, and leadership. Despite personal trials and the challenges of Church life, she remained a steady influence. After Joseph's death, Emma chose not to follow Brigham Young west, instead staying in Nauvoo with her children. Her legacy remains significant, especially in her example of faith and resilience.

Oliver Cowdery

Articulate. Devoted. Principled.

Oliver Cowdery was a close associate of Joseph Smith and played a key role in the early days of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. He served as the principal scribe during the translation of the Book of Mormon and was one of the Three Witnesses who testified of its divine origin. Cowdery was also present with Joseph Smith when they received the priesthood from heavenly messengers - a foundational event in Church history. He helped establish Church structure. Although he was excommunicated in 1838 due to disagreements, he later reconciled with the Church and was rebaptized before his death in 1850. His contributions remain foundational to the Restoration.

Martin Harris

Sincere. Generous. Steadfast.

Martin Harris was an early supporter of Joseph Smith and one of the Three Witnesses of the Book of Mormon. A prosperous farmer from New York, he provided financial support for the publication of the Book of Mormon, even mortgaging his farm to help fund the printing. Harris served briefly as a scribe during the translation and was entrusted with the first 116 manuscript pages, which were later lost—a deeply significant event in early Church history. Despite moments of doubt and eventual excommunication, he remained a believer in the Book of Mormon's divine origin. Later in life, he reunited with the Church and moved to Utah, where he bore repeated testimony of his witness before passing away in 1875.

Hyrum Smith

Loyal. Brave. Trustworthy.

Hyrum Smith was the older brother of Joseph Smith and a devoted leader in the early Church. Known for his unwavering loyalty and deep faith, he served in several key roles, including as Patriarch to the Saints. Hyrum was closely involved in the leadership and spiritual guidance of the early Saints, often standing beside Joseph during times of persecution and trial. He was also one of the Eight Witnesses of the Book of Mormon. His life came to a tragic end alongside Joseph's when both were martyred in Carthage Jail in 1844. Hyrum's legacy is remembered for his courage, humility, and steadfast support of the Restoration.

Brigham Young

Resolute. Resourceful. Determined.

Brigham Young was a pivotal leader in the early history of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints and the second President of the Church. A skilled organizer and powerful speaker, he joined the Church in 1832 and quickly rose to leadership as a member of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles. After the death of Joseph Smith in 1844, Brigham played a central role in guiding the Saints through a difficult period of transition and eventually led the historic migration west to the Salt Lake Valley in 1847. Under his leadership, the Church established thriving settlements throughout the Intermountain West. Brigham's legacy includes both the physical foundations of modern Utah and the firm establishment of the Church in the American West.
